# Source Locality

Use this when a deck contains audience-specific prices, availability, retail, regulations, schedules, events, or market claims.

## Decide the locality first

1. Infer the target market from the user's language, stated audience, organization, currency, and distribution context.
2. Record the chosen market internally before research. Do not default to the United States because English pages are easier to find.
3. If the market is ambiguous and the claim would materially change by region, either use the user's locale as the working assumption and label it or ask.

## Source order

For region-dependent claims, use this order:

1. The brand's official site for the target region and language.
2. Target-region government, regulator, exchange, public institution, or official local distributor.
3. Reputable target-region trade, research, retail, or news source.
4. Global or foreign official sources only as secondary context.

Never substitute a foreign price, launch date, stock status, promotion, law, rating, or market metric for the target region. If local data is unavailable, say so and label any foreign comparison with its country and date.

## Global technical exception

For technical standards, open-source software, cloud services, Kubernetes, protocols, and architecture behavior, prefer the canonical upstream specification or vendor documentation. Use a regional page only when localization, hosting region, certification, pricing, support, or law changes the answer.

## Slide and notes checks

- Use the target currency, date format, product name, and official local terminology.
- Put the locality in the slide title or label when confusion is possible.
- Keep the exact source URL and checked date in notes or provenance comments.
- When comparing regions, lead with the audience's region and explain why the comparison helps.
